Augmented Dance Education

Bachelor of Industrial Design Thesis

Overview

As other sports and recreational activities receive an increasing amount of support for the health and safety of athletes, dancers often receive little to none. There is a common presumption that dancing is merely a leisurely activity; however, professional dancers frequently require extensive physical therapy due to high injury incidence rates.

This thesis proposes a solution to help deter the occurrence rate of injuries in young professional dancers through augmented education, combining wearable technology with a smart environment.

Research & Problem Definition

The Problem

Dance involves many repetitive motions that can cause injuries to cascade over time. Unless measures are taken to reduce these rates, many young dancers face long-term physical consequences.

Key Insight: Dancers often lack real-time feedback on their bio-mechanics during practice, leading to poor form habits that cause injury.

User Research

Interviews with dancers and instructors revealed that while instructors correct form, they cannot be everywhere at once. Dancers practicing alone often reinforce bad habits unknowingly.

User Personas

To understand the needs of the target audience, specific personas were developed representing the young professional dancer and the dance instructor.

Empathy & Journey Mapping

Mapping the dancer's journey through a typical practice session highlighted key pain points where fatigue and lack of supervision led to injury risks.

The Solution

Wearable Feedback

Smart leggings embedded with sensors track muscle engagement and alignment in real-time. This wearable technology provides haptic feedback to the dancer, correcting posture before injuries occur.

Smart Environment

A companion floor panel system works in tandem with the wearables, offering visual cues and pressure sensing to ensure proper foot placement and weight distribution during practice.

Design Process

1. Ergonomics & Human Factors

1:1 scale studies were conducted to ensure the wearables fit diverse body types without restricting movement. The placement of sensors was critical to capture accurate muscle data.

2. Analysis & Simulation

This project focused on conceptual research and formal development. Fusion360 was used for hard-surface modeling of the floor panels, while CLO3D was utilized to simulate the fabric physics and fit of the smart leggings.

3. Prototyping

Physical study models were developed to test the ergonomics, modularity, and durability of the system. This project was a conceptual exploration of future dance education technology and did not involve functional electronics prototyping.

Final Design

The final system consists of the "Cutters Must" wearable and the modular floor panel system.
